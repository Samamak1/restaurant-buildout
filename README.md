# Tapas and Tequila Restaurant Launch Program

> A restaurant launch case study covering buildout, licensing, vendors, menu and POS systems, hiring, training, operating design, and opening readiness.

I served as **Director of Operations / Launch Consultant** for the Wichita, Kansas concept. I was **not the owner or founder**. My mandate was to coordinate the operating workstreams, build the systems required for launch, and prepare the team to sustain high-volume service after opening.

## Business challenge

Every launch workstream had to converge on the same operating date. A menu item was not ready unless its price, POS button, ingredients, station, vendor supply, trained employee, and service standard were ready together.

The concept combined construction and inspection dependencies, a large tequila program, new systems, a newly recruited team, and high expected service volume. The work was not a collection of disconnected tasks; it was management of the handoffs between them.

## My mandate and decision rights

I owned or coordinated:

## Delivery system

### 1. Opening roadmap

The opening ran through a Monday.com Gantt covering:

- Interior and exterior setup
- Post-construction inspection and cleanup
- Licensing and permits
- Key-employee hiring
- Logo and brand work
- Menu development
- Inventory and payroll systems
- Toast POS setup
- Table-reservation system
- Training and language manuals
- Employee scheduling
- Soft opening and grand opening

The public export has owner names and sensitive fields removed.

### 2. Menu engineering

The menu was engineered around daypart, service speed, and margin:

- Lunch: 11:00a-3:00p
- Weekend brunch: 11:00a-3:00p
- Tapas: 10:30a-9:00p
- Dinner: 4:00p-9:00p
- Late menu: 9:00p-close

Protein add-on ladders created a consistent upsell path across multiple dishes:

```text
Chicken +6 | Shrimp +8 | Salmon or Birria +12 | Steak +15 | Lobster +28
```

The assortment ranged from low-cost tapas anchors to premium entrees. Vegetarian, limited-availability, and late-menu indicators were built into the printed menu so operating constraints were visible to both guests and staff.

The menu moved through handwritten drafts, dated revisions, and final typeset pages. Brand-bearing drafts withheld from the public copy are transcribed only where appropriate.

### 3. Bar and dining-room design

The layout diagrams were working operating documents:

- A V-shaped bar with East and West rails
- Five positions: two bartenders, a point bartender, and two bar backs
- Station-level task definitions for tickets, pours, stocking, and drink running
- Dining-room table numbering, banquettes, patio gates, host station, kitchen entrance, and three server POS terminals

These drawings informed staffing, section assignment, seat rotation, and service-flow decisions before opening.

### 4. Pricing and POS

Bottle and pour pricing was pre-computed before Toast entry. The model supported:

- Markup options from 2x-10x in half-step increments
- 1 oz, 1.5 oz, 2 oz, and 2.5 oz pours
- Neat, rocks, cocktail, martini, and bottle-service decisions
- Product modifiers and protein upsell ladders
- Product-mix and sales reporting used for later pricing review

The supporting workbooks are documented in the companion [`alcohol-pricing-engine`](https://github.com/Samamak1/alcohol-pricing-engine) repository.

### 5. People, vendors, and operating infrastructure

- Recruited bartenders, servers, chefs, and line cooks through Indeed
- Built an employee handbook, uniform standards, and training and language materials
- Implemented Sling scheduling and payroll/time-clock workflows
- Coordinated liquor distributors, seafood supply, smallwares, and other launch vendors
- Prepared the team for soft opening, gathered feedback, and corrected readiness gaps before full-volume operation
